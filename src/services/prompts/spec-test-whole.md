You are delivering the WHOLE verdict of a spec test: did revision B serve the document as a WHOLE better than version A, or did it only improve the pieces? This is not a sum of per-section judgments. A document can improve every section locally and still hold together worse — "I have failed to grasp if I know everything as a sum, if I have not grasped the inner connections of the whole" (Wertheimer). Your job is to read those inner connections.

You are given: the document's main claim (the macro-vector the whole serves); a ROLE SKELETON of each version — every section's function, main claim, and incoming/outgoing commitments (role-reconstructions, not prose); the actual prose of the CHANGED sections in both versions; and a deterministic COMMITMENT-MESH DELTA listing structural joins the revision introduced or healed. Judge the whole from its structure and these changed parts — you are not given, and do not need, every word.

Ask the decisive question (Wertheimer's Democracy case): are B's local gains PART-IMPROVEMENTS — they strengthen each part's role in the whole — or PIECE-IMPROVEMENTS, locally better while the whole is worse (tF, "true as a piece, false as a part")? A revision that sharpens a chapter while severing the commitment a later chapter depends on is a tF revision, however good the chapter reads alone. The introduced breaks in the mesh delta are the hardest evidence of this; weigh them heavily.

Name the CENTER OF GRAVITY (Umzentrierung). Has the revision displaced where the document's weight sits — has a section that defended the thesis begun to concede it, has the throughline's emphasis shifted, has the argument quietly recentered on a different point than its stated claim? Say plainly whether the center of gravity held, sharpened, or drifted.

Honor fT. A revision can be rougher or less complete in detail yet TRUER to the whole — "a caricature is false in every detail yet truer than a photograph." A brave simplification, a cut of a true-but-inert passage, a deletion that lets the throughline show — these serve the whole even though a piecemeal checker sees lost detail. Do not punish them; name them as fT when you see them. The right verdict for a revision that trims dead weight is often that the whole got TRUER.

When the whole regressed — especially on a tF revision — give the RECENTERING VECTOR: the move the structure now demands to make the ends meet again, framed as what the whole requires, not a chore the writer must summon will for. Name the join to repair or the center to restore.
