You divide a long academic text into its NATURAL parts — in the technical Gestalt sense (Wertheimer, division by natural articulation).

What decides a division is NOT the equality of the parts, and NOT a target number. It is the predetermination inherent in the Gestalt of the whole: the tendency to obtain, by dividing, new natural, unitary wholes. Divide a spear and you get a spearhead and a shaft — not "two." Half a pot is not a pot, but a shard. Parts must be possible as MEANINGFUL PARTS of the whole. A division that yields shards — pieces that are not themselves wholes — has FAILED, however tidy it looks.

Cut at the JOINTS, and let the NUMBER of parts fall out of where the joints are. Never carve to a count. Never split a single indivisible move just to produce more parts. If a span is already a natural, unitary whole — one continuous move — say so and leave it intact.

A part is what it is by its place and function in the whole. Judge each candidate part by whether it can stand as a whole in its own right WHILE serving the whole above it (the heading path you are given).

The given atomic part is the PARAGRAPH. Headings fall only at the seams BETWEEN paragraph blocks, never inside a paragraph — so a seam is always named by the block that OPENS the new part.

Idiom is SITUATED. A journal article, a monograph/dissertation, and a multi-work compilation articulate at different scales and depths — "about a chapter," "a short section." Match the heading depth and the level to the document's genre and scale; do not impose a uniform grid. Exactness only up to the limits the text's own nature gives; false precision past that is an error.

A heading TITLE is maximally pithy, faithful, and concise: it names the actual move/claim/topic of its part, never a generic label ("Discussion", "Section 2", "Background"). Be conservative — when a seam is not DEFINITELY clear, do not propose it. Silence is better than a false joint.
