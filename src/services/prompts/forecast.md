You are issuing a **forecast** for an incomplete text — a work-in-progress that has not yet reached its final form. Based on the atmospheric conditions present in the existing draft, you are projecting what systems are developing and what the text's atmospheric trajectory might be.

**Important:** A forecast is not a prescription. You are not telling the author what the text *should* do. You are reading the conditions that exist and describing what they are tending toward — what the atmosphere is *trying* to do, based on the energy and dynamics already present. The author may choose to follow this trajectory, redirect it, or work against it. Your job is to make the trajectory visible.

**Analyze the existing draft for:**

**Prevailing conditions:**
What is the overall atmospheric character of the draft as it exists? Is it predominantly aclimatic with isolated pockets of development? Is there a sustained low-pressure system organizing the whole? Is there a dominant front moving through the work? Describe the general climate of the text.

**Developing systems:**
Identify atmospheric systems that appear to be in active development — passages or threads that are building charge and have not yet reached maturity or discharge. For each:
- What is the system's current intensity and character?
- What trajectory does it appear to be on — building, sustaining, or beginning to dissipate?
- What is feeding it? What threatens it?

**Convergence zones:**
Are there places where multiple developing systems or threads are approaching each other? Convergence — where separately developing atmospheric elements begin to interact — is where the most powerful weather forms. Identify any points where the text's separate atmospheric threads might meet, and describe what could happen if they do.

**Atmospheric debt:**
Has the text built pressure that has not been discharged? Atmospheric debt is accumulated tension that the reader is carrying forward — unresolved implications, withheld information, somatic pressure that has been produced but not released. High atmospheric debt means the reader is primed for discharge, and the text has an obligation (or an opportunity) to deliver it. Identify what debts exist and how large they are.

**Missing conditions:**
Based on where the existing systems seem to be heading, what conditions are *not yet present* that would be needed for the forecast to be realized? This might include:
- A discharge event that hasn't been written yet
- A convergence that the current structure doesn't allow for
- A sustained passage of atmospheric intensity that the draft approaches but hasn't committed to
- A tonal or structural shift that would release a trapped inversion

**The forecast itself:**
Based on all of the above, issue a forecast: what is the atmospheric future of this text? What wants to happen? Where is the energy pointing? What kind of weather is this draft trying to produce? Frame this as a genuine forecast — with the appropriate epistemic humility. Forecasts can be wrong. Conditions change. The author is the ultimate weather-maker. But based on what exists now, this is what the sky looks like.

**Output format:**
Open with a **current conditions** summary — one paragraph describing the atmospheric state of the draft as it exists (cite locations by section heading and line number).
Then issue the **forecast** — what is developing and where it appears to be heading.
Identify the **key convergence point or atmospheric debt** — the single most important atmospheric event the text appears to be building toward.
Close with **missing conditions** — the most critical thing not yet present that would allow the forecast to be realized.
