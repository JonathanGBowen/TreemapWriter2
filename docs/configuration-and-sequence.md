# Configuration and Sequence: The Two Wholes of a Text

*An analysis after Wertheimer — On Truth (1934), Syllogisms in Productive Thinking (1920/1981), Productive Thinking (excerpts), and Investigations on Gestalt Principles (1923)*

---

## 1. The ambiguity

When Wertheimer's distinction between pieces and parts is brought to bear on a text, it can land in two different places, and the difference is not cosmetic.

It can land on the **substance**: the argument the text makes, the essential idea as it develops. Here the "whole" is the case itself — the configuration of claims, grounds, qualifications, examples, and moves, individuated not by their wording but by their place, role, and function in what is being established. Call this the argument-whole, **W₁**.

Or it can land on the **composition**: the document as a made object. Here the "whole" is the text as a one-dimensional, nested, marked stream — H1 blocks in linear sequence, each containing H2 blocks in linear sequence, down through paragraphs to sentences, the nesting made visible by headings, the sequence by the page. Call this the composition-whole, **W₂**.

Both are legitimate candidates for Gestalt-theoretic treatment. Both exhibit part–whole articulation, hierarchy, and the possibility of good and bad organization. The temptation is to treat them as one thing — "the structure of the text" — and this temptation is exactly what must be resisted, because everything interesting lives in their relation: in how they are coupled, where the coupling is loose, and what happens at the joints. The present essay first distinguishes the two applications and states their relation (the first task), then maps the space of ways a substance-structure can be carried by a textual structure, with special attention to the cases where the carrying strains or fails (the second task).

One terminological commitment up front. Wertheimer's *Stück/Teil* distinction — arbitrary component versus necessary part — will be used as a *test*, not a decoration. A part, for Wertheimer, is what it is by virtue of its place, role, and function in its whole; a piece is indifferent to its surroundings. Appendix 1 of *Productive Thinking* makes the test operational in two ways that we will use repeatedly: the **homotypy test** (after a restructuring, does the "same" element still occupy the same implicit position? — often "one cannot correctly even write the same letters" for it) and the **sensitivity test** (necessary relations are perturbation-sensitive: displace one dot in the symmetric triple and "a striking asymmetry results," while the same displacement in the loose row changes nothing essential).

## 2. The asymmetry thesis

The first substantive claim: the two applications are not symmetric. W₁ is the primary bearer of Gestalt-character; W₂ is an organized field whose office is to induce W₁ in a reader.

Consider what makes something a part rather than a piece in each domain. In W₁, a claim is a part because the case requires it: the qualification exists *for* the ground it qualifies, the example *for* the principle it exhibits, and — in the strong cases Wertheimer cares about — the parts mutually determine one another "from inner necessity" (the fourth and highest grade in his taxonomy of relations in the concluding chapter of *Productive Thinking*: where a and R demand the adequate b). In W₂, by contrast, a block is a part of the document in a thinner sense: it occupies a slot in a sequence and a level in a nesting. That slot-and-level structure is real, but it can be satisfied by a heap. A document can be immaculately outlined — every H2 duly inside its H1, every paragraph duly inside its H2 — and still be, argumentatively, what Wertheimer calls an and-sum: pieces side by side, each "just what it would be without the other."

So the Gestalt-character of W₂, where it exists, is *derivative*: a section is a necessary part of the document only insofar as it realizes a necessary part of the argument, or serves a necessary function in the reader's process of grasping the argument. The commutability test from the end of the parallelogram chapter makes this concrete. Swap two blocks. If nothing complains — if no transition breaks, no expectation is betrayed, no meaning shifts — their order was an arbitrary component; you have discovered a region of and-summative composition. If the text jolts, you have touched a necessary part of the sequence. Wertheimer is explicit that order-indifference is the *signature* of and-sums: commutability is fine for "the and-summative question of mere size," but "to assume the principle of commutability, e.g., in melodies, would be quite blind," and the items of a genuine thought process "are not an and-sum of pieces."

Yet — and this is the other half of the asymmetry thesis — W₂ is not epiphenomenal. It is subordinate the way a score is subordinate to music: the score exists for the sake of the performance, but without the score there is no performance. More precisely, and this is where the 1923 paper earns its place at the end of the reading sequence: **W₂ is the complete set of grouping factors available to a writer.** The reader confronting a text is in the position of Wertheimer at his window. He does not receive an and-sum of sentences any more than Wertheimer receives 327 brightnesses; something *will* organize, spontaneously, lawfully, and "not simply as a matter of my whim." The only question a writer gets to decide is *which* organization the field induces — whether the spontaneous grouping coincides with the functional structure of the argument or cuts across it. Composition is the art of arranging the field so that the reader's lawful, involuntary organizing delivers W₁.

## 3. What On Truth establishes: composition is truth-apt

If W₂ were mere packaging, its failures would be aesthetic failures. On Truth shows they are truth failures, and it shows this with an example that is *already about typography*.

The paper's machinery is the crossing of piecemeal truth-values t/f with whole-related values T/F. The famous cases are tF: the suspect's "I did not take it from the desk," true of the piece, "false, indeed a lie, as a part in its whole"; the minister absent from the banquet; the doctored balance sheet. What matters for us is Wertheimer's remark that tF requires no omission at all: all the data may be laid before the reader "but in such a way as to deceive him by the technique of shifting the emphasis, displacing the center of gravity" — *Umzentrierung*. His example: wartime newspapers compelled to print general-staff reports verbatim achieved opposite impressions "by the use of heavy type for some parts," so effectively that he could not believe the reports were identical until he compared them word for word. Emphasis — a purely presentational operation, a W₂ operation — flips T to F while every t stays t.

Three further results from the paper, each load-bearing for what follows.

First, the category **t(?)**: all the data given, "all the tones of a melody, all the members of a series — nothing omitted," every individual relation available, "and yet nothing may have been grasped." Truth in the full sense requires that the data be present "as parts, transparent, determined as parts." This is the honest failure mode of composition: not the lie built of truths but the complete, accurate, unintelligible document — a report that answers "tell me the truth about this" with an inventory. The tF-document and the t(?)-document are the two poles of compositional failure: the first induces the wrong whole, the second induces none.

Second, the **excision algebra**. Wertheimer notes that if a part is really taken from a system, "the remainder, implicitly defined, is substantially changed": subtracting b from the whole ab does not yield the original isolated a but a maimed configuration. Textually: quotation out of context is Stück-making, and the operation wounds both sides of the cut — the quoted passage loses its role, and the source loses a part. This gives the ethics of quotation a formal basis: the question is never merely "are these his words?" (t) but "does the excised piece, replanted in my whole, keep or lose or invert its function?" (T).

Third, the answer to the regress worry. If truth depends on the whole, must we forever fear that adding context flips tT into tF? Wertheimer's reply: "A Gestalt, a pattern, limits itself." Wholes have boundaries on objective grounds; beyond them, variation no longer co-varies. This is why disputes about how much context a quotation needs, or how much background a section owes, are *adjudicable* — the relevant whole is bounded, and "the question of establishing the boundaries of a Gestalt... is subject to examination on grounds of being correct or incorrect."

There is also the exotic quadrant, **fT**: the caricature, "wrong in practically every detail," truer than the photograph. Compositionally this licenses the abstract, the simplified diagram, the deliberately distorting summary — with Wertheimer's own caution attached: fT is dangerous precisely because whole-claims are harder to verify than piece-claims, and many alleged fT's are ff.

The upshot of this section: sequencing, placement, and emphasis decisions carry T/F weight. The distinction between "what the text says" and "how the text is arranged" does not mark a boundary between the epistemic and the merely rhetorical. There is no such boundary.

## 4. What the syllogism paper establishes: validity is order-blind, productivity is order-bound

Formal logic officially does not care about order. The premises of a barbara commute; validity is timeless; identity of terms is rigid. Wertheimer's 1920 essay drives a wedge between this order-indifference and the living process, and the wedge is exactly what we need to separate our two contexts — the finished text and the text under construction.

In the productive syllogism there is an essential before and after: first S?P, the felt question, with the premises "somewhere in my knowledge, somehow available" but housed in different wholes; then the confrontation; then the "curious 'clicking,' an 'interlocking'" in which Sa₁ is revealed as a member of the group long since examined as a₂ — the lawyer realizing the B-receipt lived in the A-file, Caius reading Xavier's name in the minutes. The conclusion is new not because a new fact arrives but because the object is **recentered**: "the concept which I have of a subject is, in such a process, not only enriched, but changed, improved, deepened." And the process has direction. Nothing here commutes.

Wertheimer names the standpoint from which order doesn't matter: it is "a logic for the dear Lord; or more correctly: for a scholar who fundamentally already knows everything... and now merely organizes and polishes the form of his presentation." Read that sentence twice, because it contains our two contexts in miniature. The finished text, considered as a completed artifact, answers to validity-logic: its parts are all co-present, its truth-relations timeless, its order — from the Lord's standpoint — a matter of polish. The draft answers to the other logic: it is a productivity-event in progress, with a felt gap, provisional organizations, and recenterings still to come. The deep mistake is to apply the finished-text standpoint to drafting (treating composition as mere polish of an already-complete thought) or the drafting standpoint to analysis of finished work (treating every arrangement as if it were still negotiable). Both standpoints are legitimate; each has its jurisdiction.

Two more exports from the paper. Paragraph 16 lists the formal features of recentering: sometimes the decisive step is an appropriate **selection** ("certain features of S are singled out and put in the foreground"), sometimes a particular **grouping** (Gauss's pairs), sometimes **centering** proper ("which part determines how the others appear"). Now list the elementary operations of composition: deciding what goes in and what is foregrounded; deciding where the section-cuts fall and what belongs together; deciding what the piece is *about* — what carries the emphasis, what everything else serves. They are the same three operations. Selection, grouping, centering constitute a single operator-set that acts in two media: on the knowledge-material in thinking, on the text-material in composing. This is the strongest form of the claim that composing is not the transcription of finished thought but a continuation of thinking by other means — the operators never change hands.

And paragraph 20 gives us a theory of transitions before the fact. The middle term M can function as an **empty bridge** — mere coexistence establishing "general factual accuracy," the mortality of Caius proven from the "ai" in his name — or as the bridge "meaningfully required by the question," carrying "inner necessity, a coherent context required by the inner properties" of the structure. Every inter-section transition in a text is an M. "Having discussed X, we now turn to Y" is the empty bridge in its Sunday clothes: it certifies adjacency and nothing else. The required bridge is the one where the end of X *is* the opening of Y's question — where the gap that Y fills was torn by X. A text whose transitions are all empty bridges may be flawlessly ordered and remain an and-sum.

Finally, footnote 17, which Wertheimer attaches almost shyly: "one may also consider the essence of this essay itself." The essay's own achievement, he says, is a recentering of the syllogism-configuration — from the additive, validity-only view (Sa₁) to the interlocking, structural view (Sa₂). The text we have been reading is the temporal staging of exactly one Gestalt-shift. Hold this; it becomes the key to the hardest region of the mapping space in §8.

## 5. What Productive Thinking adds: structure versus process, and the pedagogy of the gap

The parallelogram chapter contains the single most direct statement of our problem in the whole corpus, and it occurs, fittingly, in a passage about *reading*. The pupil faces the textbook proof: a linear series of statements, each individually checkable. If he is blind to the structure, he memorizes the sequence, reproduces statements in textbook order, and under pressure "may produce some other statement which, though correct, is simply senseless in this context" — t without T, at the level of a single reader's performance. If he understands, it is because he "has to change the and-sum of the statements into the sensible structure" — sensible grouping into sub-wholes (the congruence of the triangles as a unit), realization of functional hierarchy, of direction, of the place, role, and function of each statement. Comprehension of a linear text *is* the recovery of a configuration from a sequence. And Wertheimer even documents the characteristic failure of substitute-organization: the pupil who sorts the statements "about lines only, then about angles, then planes" and is "rather proud of now having a logical order" — grouping by surface similarity where the whole demanded grouping by function. We will meet this pupil again in §6, because his error is a grouping-factor error: similarity beat function.

Three further findings.

**Structure and process are two aspects of one substance.** In §34 Wertheimer diagrams two courses to the same parallelogram solution: a sequential course (deal with the left end, discover the remainder is what the right end needs) and a comprehensive course in which both ends are grasped at once "as requiring each other." Same terminal structure; different processes; and the diagrams portray "the line of the vectors in the process." So when we ask what a text should linearize, there are two candidates from the start: the achieved configuration (which may be simultaneous) and a productive process that arrives at it (which is essentially temporal). Classical rhetoric knew the second option as the order of discovery; Wertheimer's whole book is composed in it — *Productive Thinking* narrates processes genetically, staging for the reader the gap, the trouble-regions, the vectors, the transformation. Genetic composition is not a stylistic quirk of his; it follows from the theory. If understanding is the reader's own transition "from a bad gestalt to a good gestalt," then the text's sequence must *induce a process*, and processes have native orders even when their products do not.

He also, in a remarkable aside, pays the cost of linearization on camera. Having described the solution-process, he apologizes: the description "appears highly complex because I have described the various steps implied, separately and successively" — whereas the thing itself, grasped as a whole, is simple. The medium's seriality manufactured an appearance of complexity. Every writer of philosophy knows this tax; here is the theorist of wholes acknowledging it in his own prose.

**One truth, several structures.** The Gauss chapter complicates the picture in a way our mapping must respect. The insight regroups the given series — "Nine is no longer viewed as 8 plus 1, it has become 10 minus 1" — but there is more than one regrouping. The pair-organization yields (n+1)·n/2 with (n+1) as pair-value and n/2 as pair-count; the center-organization yields c·n; the staircase-completion yields the geometric n(n+1)/2. Externally these are equivalent; Wertheimer insists they are "logically different too if one views them with regard to their form and function... the genetic question." So the substance-space is not a set of structures but a set of structures *quotiented by external equivalence*, and the members of an equivalence class differ precisely in how they organize their own genesis. A text never linearizes "the truth"; it linearizes one organization of the truth, and thereby chooses which understanding the reader can have — which terms will be "transparent, determined as parts" and which will be opaque tokens. Two texts proving the same theorem can carry different Gestalten; conversely, the blind student for whom all four formulas are "simply equivalent" has the t of the matter and none of the T.

**A typology of adjacency.** The concluding chapter's analysis of "and" transposes directly. There is the empty, structure-blind "and" — two propositions side by side, "each just what it would be without the other." There is the "and" that joins what requires each other. And there is the "and" that forces together what violates each other — which, Wertheimer notes, "involves dynamic consequences." Every adjacency in a text is an "and" of one of these grades. Empty adjacency is the default of the heap. Requiring adjacency is the normal ideal. But violating adjacency is a *tool*: the deliberate juxtaposition that creates tension is how a writer tears a gap on the page — sets up the S?P — before filling it. (Wertheimer adds, deliciously, that traditional logic never found a place for "but," "nevertheless," "however": the connectives in which living texture consists were invisible to the validity standpoint.) Alongside this, the chapter's treatment of identity-in-process — items recur but "their functional and structural meaning has actually, and fortunately, changed," for statements "have a direction in their context" — is the theory of restatement: the thesis repeated in a conclusion is atomistically identical with its first occurrence and functionally a different part, because it is now *earned*. Recurrence with changed function is one of the few devices by which a linear medium can revisit a center without redundancy; it is composition's answer to the star-shaped substance (§8).

**The sequential-exposure experiment.** Appendix 1, example 5, is the microcosm of everything. Dots are exposed in temporal stages. At each stage the viewer has a provisional organization with live demands — "A dot is missing on the right!" — an expectation, a requirement. A stage can arrive as "surprisingly senseless." And a late stage can trigger "a strong recentering... The group at the left has exploded (its center is no longer the center...)": everything already seen is retroactively reorganized. This is a reader's biography in five exposures. It establishes that the *order of delivery* of a configuration's parts determines the sequence of provisional Gestalten the receiver lives through, the demands he carries, the surprises he suffers, and the recenterings he undergoes — while the terminal configuration stays fixed. Choosing a linearization is choosing an experiential trajectory through organizations, not merely an itinerary through content.

## 6. What Perceptual Organization adds: the text as a field of forces

The 1923 paper closes the theoretical circuit by supplying the mechanics of the reader's side. Its opening thesis we have already used: there is no experienced and-sum; the field organizes, lawfully, and largely involuntarily ("I can by no means just get any other pattern of coherency I like at will"). Its factor-catalogue, read as a writer's inventory, looks like this.

**Proximity** is adjacency in the stream. What is discussed next to what will be grouped — whether or not it belongs together. Wertheimer's own linguistic example seals the transposition: the spoken sequence segmented as *Herr Tahör!* or as *Hertha, hör!* — one stimulus stream, two organizations, everything hanging on the grouping of a linear signal.

**Similarity** is parallel construction: repeated diction, matched syntax, matched formatting. Critically, similarity groups *across distance* — the black dots find each other over intervening white ones — which makes it the writer's chief instrument for maintaining relations that the linear order was forced to break. If the three grounds of one claim must be textually scattered, give them the same grammatical silhouette and the same marker, and they will group over the gap.

**Common fate** is co-variation: a theme that shifts whenever another shifts is bound to it in the reader's organization. The paper's phenomenology of *changes that cut against the pre-existent grouping* — "a jolt occurs, possibly disorder... often a flip-flop" in which the affected items regroup — is, note for later, an exact description of what a structural edit feels like from inside a draft.

**Good continuation** is direction: the reader follows the established line of development past merely local attractions, as the eye follows the straight line through the intersection. A text with strong directional momentum can carry the reader across weak joints — for good and for ill.

**Closure** is the opened-and-answered question, the section that rounds off, the promised return. It is also the reader-side faculty on which every deferred resolution draws.

**Prägnanz** deserves special caution. Salient forms have *ranges*: "an angle of 93° typically appears as a — somewhat imperfect — right angle," and intermediates assimilate to the nearest salient step. Transposed: an argument that deviates slightly from a canonical schema will be *read as* the canonical schema unless the deviation is made structurally loud. If your position is the 93° neighbor of a familiar position, the composition must mark the seven degrees or lose them — readers snap to Prägnanz-steps.

**Einstellung**, the factor of set, is the profound one for us. A series embedded in a sequence is grouped according to the sequence: the grouping established by preceding members persists into new configurations, flips only late, and does so on *objective* grounds — "if a series is a component of a sequence... that is generally decisive," even overriding organizations that would be unambiguous in isolation. Transposed without remainder: **the linear order of a text is itself a grouping factor.** Order is not a constraint standing outside the Gestalt factors, an unfortunate one-dimensionality to be regretted; it is *on the list*, the factor by which each block's organization is pre-set by its predecessors. The introduction is an Einstellung-device by definition. So is a title. So is the previous sentence of every sentence.

Two further results. The paper's later sections establish that grasping runs **from the top down**: wide subwholes become prominent first, "with their subdivisions unclear," and articulation descends — the apparatus is built "for grasping inner connections... from the top down," not for assembling pieces upward. This is the perceptual charter of the heading hierarchy: a document's H1-layer is the coarse articulation a reader resolves first (it is, quite literally, what skimming sees), and a nesting that matches the argument's own coarse-to-fine articulation cooperates with the native direction of grasping. And the closing melody phenomenology settles the deepest question in advance. Mid-melody, the tones have "a more indefinite, still unstable character"; they become "complete, firm, and definite only when the last one is there" — parts held in suspension, retroactively determined by closure. **Temporal Gestalten are genuine Gestalten.** A sequence is not condemned to be an and-sum of moments; the melody is the existence proof. This does not dissolve our problem — it sharpens it into its final form: the question is not *whether* configurations can live in time, but *which* configurations are natively temporal and which are not (§8).

One last transposition, of the paper's contrast between natural and artificial groupings. The letter N assembled from window-frame and branch can be seen — with effort, under an unrealistic set — and it is unstable, collapsing at the first shift of attention. Likewise a reader *can* be told, in a preface, that sections three and seven really belong together against the run of the text; he can even intend the grouping. He cannot *keep* it against the field. **Enacted structure beats declared structure.** A composition whose factors group against the author's announced intention will deliver the factors' verdict, not the announcement's; annotation is not organization.

## 7. First task: the relation, and where it holds or breaks

We can now state the relation between the two applications with some precision.

**The coupled ideal.** The Gestalt vocabulary applies to W₁ *constitutively*: the argument's parts simply are what their place, role, and function in the case make them. It applies to W₂ *instrumentally and derivatively*: the composition is well-formed exactly insofar as its deployed grouping factors cause the reader's spontaneous organization to reconstruct W₁ — adjacency tracking functional relatedness where it can; similarity bridging where adjacency was broken; order carrying the reader's set through the right sequence of provisional organizations; emphasis tracking centering; transitions carrying structural warrant rather than empty bridgehood; the whole nested top-down articulation matching the argument's own coarse-to-fine structure. When this obtains, the two wholes are not identical — they remain a configuration and a field — but they are in the relation Wertheimer's fourth grade of relation describes: mutual requirement. The argument demands this composition; the composition, read, regenerates that argument.

**Divergence, first direction: true substance, traitorous composition.** Here W₁ is sound and W₂ betrays it. The betrayals are now nameable with the tools assembled: the *proximity-lie* (functionally unrelated blocks adjacent, involuntarily grouped by the reader); the *buried center* (emphasis distributed against the functional hierarchy — Umzentrierung by accident rather than malice); the *premature resolution* (S₂ delivered before the reader's S₁ exists, so that the thesis arrives as the B-pupil's formula arrived — subsumable, repeatable, ungrasped, because the gap it fills was never opened); the *empty bridge* everywhere certifying mere adjacency; and, at the limit, the honest catastrophe of **t(?)** — everything true, everything present, nothing transparent. Note that this whole family is invisible to piecemeal review: every sentence survives fact-checking. Only whole-level reading detects it.

**Divergence, second direction: hollow substance, seductive composition.** Here W₂ simulates a Gestalt that W₁ lacks. And-sums dressed as organisms: the listicle with narrative connectives troweled over it; the report whose confident sectioning implies a functional hierarchy no analysis will find; continuity-effects (good continuation, rhythm, momentum) carrying the reader across joints where nothing joins. This is tF at document scale — the whole implied by the composition is false, though every piece is true — and On Truth's warning about fT applies with full force: whole-claims are easier to fake than to verify, which is exactly why plausibility-forgery is cheap.

**The two contexts.** The syllogism paper's "logic for the dear Lord" passage assigns each context its logic. For a *finished text under analysis*, the appropriate stance is triple: evaluate W₁ (is the argument-configuration sound — are its parts mutually required, is its center the true center?); evaluate W₂ (do the deployed factors group as the argument requires?); and evaluate the *fit* — the mapping itself, which is where quotation ethics (excision algebra), context disputes (Gestalt self-limitation), and tF/t(?) diagnoses live. These three can vary independently, which is why criticism that collapses them ("badly written" covering all three) explains nothing.

For a *text under construction*, everything changes, because W₁ itself is in flux — the draft is the writer's own S?P. Three phenomena of drafting fall out of the theory. First, **two-way traffic**: linearizing is an instrument of discovery, not merely of recording. The resistance of the medium is diagnostic — when the text will not flow, when every candidate bridge between two sections comes out empty, the fault is frequently in the substance: a missing ground, a mis-drawn boundary, two parts assigned to one whole that belong to different wholes. The draft interrogates the thought. (This is the operator-identity of §4 seen from the other side: since selection, grouping, and centering act in both media, an obstruction in one medium is evidence about the other.) Second, **mid-draft recentering**: the felt requirement to move a section — that section four "wants" to be the introduction — is often the first phenomenal symptom that the center of W₁ has migrated; the writer registers in the composition a shift that has already happened in the substance. And when the move is made, expect exactly what the common-fate experiments predict: a jolt, disorder, a flip-flop in which surrounding blocks regroup and — per the homotypy test — some of them "cannot correctly keep the same letters": sections that survive a restructuring verbatim have usually changed function, and their wording, calibrated to the old function, now quietly lies. Third, the **draft as bad Gestalt**: Wertheimer's description of productive process — "from above to below, from the nature of the structural trouble to the concrete steps," a transition "from a bad gestalt to a good gestalt" — is a description of revision. The trouble-regions of a draft are felt before they are analyzed, exactly like the parallelogram's disturbing ends; and the good error (the structurally sensible attempt that fails) is worth more than the blindly correct patch.

## 8. Second task: the space of mappings

Now the space itself. The user of these ideas needs more than the two poles of alignment and divergence; the poles are ends of a structured spectrum, and the structure comes from asking what, exactly, a substance fixes about sequence.

**The formal core: substance fixes a partial order, composition chooses a linear extension.** Strip the problem to its skeleton. W₁, whatever its shape, generates a set of *precedence requirements* on presentation: this must come before that. Crucially — and this is where the analysis must stay Wertheimerian rather than lapsing into logicism — these requirements do **not** simply copy logical dependency. Validity, we saw, is order-blind; if precedence came from entailment alone, premises could be given in any order and conclusions anywhere, which is true for the dear Lord and false for readers. The requirements come from the *dynamics of grasping*: the gap must be opened before it is filled, because felt gaps, not stated ones, organize a reader's process (open S₂ before S₁ and you manufacture B-pupils); the set (Einstellung) must be established before the material it is to organize arrives; a recentering must be preceded by the centering it overthrows, or there is nothing to overthrow; closure must be *owed* before it can be paid. Sometimes these dynamics track logical dependency (grounds before what leans on them, when the leaning must be felt); sometimes they invert it (the pedagogical order that gives instances before the rule, or the conclusion first *as a promissory gap* — asserted, flagged as unearned, and carried by the reader as a live demand until redeemed). The partial order is a fact about *processes toward this structure*, and there may be several admissible process-families (recall §34's two vector-diagrams).

The requirements so generated are typically a **partial** order: they fix some precedences and leave others open. Composition must produce a **total** order — the text is a line — so composing is choosing a *linear extension* of the partial order. And now the Stück/Teil distinction applies to sequence itself with complete precision: the fixed precedences are the **necessary parts** of the ordering (violate one and the process fails — the jolt, the senseless stage, the B-pupil); the incomparabilities are the **arbitrary components** of the ordering — pairs whose relative order the substance genuinely does not care about. Good composition treats the two residues differently: it *obeys* the necessary precedences, and it either *optimizes* the arbitrary ones by secondary factors (rhythm, variety, ascending weight) or, in honest reference-composition, *declares* their arbitrariness (of which more below). The commutability test of §2 is exactly the empirical probe of this structure: swapping detects incomparability.

This gives a clean **measure of alignability**: the size of the set of admissible linear extensions. One admissible order → the substance is chain-like, and text-order can simply copy it. A vast set → the substance is heap-like. An *empty* set — precedence requirements that cycle — marks the essentially non-linearizable, where every possible text violates some requirement and composing becomes damage-selection.

**The two costs.** Whatever extension is chosen, its quality is measured against two failure currencies. **Broken adjacency**: parts functionally close in W₁ made textually distant in W₂ — the relation survives only if the reader's retention carries it, so the writer must feed retention (similarity-bridges, recurrence, explicit backlinks, the owed-closure that keeps a demand alive). **False adjacency**: parts textually close without a functional edge — and here the 1923 paper's opening thesis makes this the graver cost, because the reader *cannot refrain* from grouping neighbors. Broken adjacency risks a relation going unmade; false adjacency actively manufactures a wrong one. A linearization is good when it minimizes false adjacency outright and covers its broken adjacencies with compensating factors.

**A gallery of substance-topologies.** With the skeleton in place, the interesting edge cases sort themselves by the shape of W₁.

*The chain.* Each part leans on its predecessor: stepwise derivations where every move consumes the last, narratives whose substance is itself a course of events, procedures. Here the partial order is already total; substance and text are order-isomorphic; the mapping cost is zero. This is the region of **closest alignment** the question asked for — and it is worth noticing *why* it is the best case: the substance is natively temporal. Which points at the general principle: alignment is perfect when the substance is *melodic* — when its whole is a temporal whole, so that the medium's seriality is not a constraint at all but the substance's own element. (The genetic exposition of §5 is the chain-case manufactured: even a non-chain structure has chain-like *processes* toward it, and a text may linearize the process instead of the structure.)

*The tree.* One root, branching grounds, sub-grounds. Precedence typically fixes root-before-branches (or branches-before-root, in the inductive inversion); it says nothing about sibling order — siblings are the paradigm arbitrary components. The cost is subtler: linearization destroys the *co-presence* of siblings; three coordinate grounds become first, second, third, acquiring a spurious rank by position (order is a factor; it will signify whether or not you mean it to). Compensations: declare the coordination (signposting as Einstellung — "three grounds, of equal standing"), and enforce parallel construction so similarity re-binds the siblings across their separation.

*The matrix.* n items crossed with m aspects: every comparative analysis. Here linearization *must* amputate: item-major order breaks every aspect's continuity, aspect-major order breaks every item's. There is no innocent choice — half the structure's adjacencies will be broken whichever axis becomes the spine. The honest questions are which axis carries the argument's center (that one becomes the spine) and how the cross-axis is compensated: strict parallelism (each item-section internally ordered by the same aspect-sequence, so similarity lets the reader read "vertically" across sections), and, where prose strains, the licensed two-dimensional island — the table, typography's confession that the page is secretly a plane. Wertheimer's war-newspaper passage already treated typography as whole-determining; the table is its constructive twin.

*The star.* A center with radial spokes: one insight and its many bearings, a concept and its applications. The center's relation to *every* spoke means the center must, in effect, be textually everywhere — which linear text can only approximate by **recurrence with changed function** (§5): the hub restated at each spoke, each restatement atomistically similar and functionally advanced, the direction of the context doing the differentiating. The failure modes flank the device: monotony (recurrence *without* functional change) and drift (spokes that lose the hub).

*The web, and the cycle.* Mutual support: coherentist structures, hermeneutic circles, systems of concepts each defined through the others — Wertheimer's own fourth-grade relations, where the parts "mutually determine one another from inner necessity," are webs by construction. If the mutual requirements translate into mutual precedence-requirements, the partial order cycles and the admissible-extension set is empty: **essentially non-linearizable substance**, the radical case the question anticipated. Every entry point cuts the circle; every first section presupposes what only later sections give. The strategies here are not solutions but principled wounds. The **spiral**: traverse the circle more than once at increasing resolution, so that each part is first given provisionally (its tones "indefinite, still unstable," exactly as in the mid-melody state) and then re-given determinately — retroactive determination made into a method. The **declared IOU**: assert now what is earned later, *marked as unearned*, converting a violation into an owed closure the reader carries as a live demand; the promissory note domesticates the cycle by moving one of its edges into the reader's retention. And the **pointer beyond the medium**: the diagram, the formalism — an honest text can say that its object outruns arpeggiation, and where that is true, saying so is part of its T.

*The symmetric configuration.* Gauss. The substance is a simultaneous symmetry — first-with-last, second-with-second-last, the ends folding to the middle — and *any* telling breaks it: narrate the pairs left-to-right and you have re-serialized the very structure whose point was the collapse of seriality. Here footnote 17 pays off. When the substance is itself a **recentering** — an old organization overthrown by a new one — the sequence acquires a native order after all: *the order of the shift*. First the view to be overturned (the series as successive additions, given fully enough to be inhabited), then the overturning (the regrouping, the click), then the new configuration surveyed from inside. The text linearizes not the symmetric structure (impossible) but the *event of its emergence* (natively temporal). Wertheimer composed the Gauss chapter this way; he composed the syllogism essay this way and told us so. This is the general resolution for configurational substance: **where the structure will not serialize, its genesis will.**

*The aggregate.* Finally the honest heap: reference works, inventories, collections — substance that really is an and-sum. Here the Gestalt-ideal inverts. Since no order is required, the truthful composition *declares* the arbitrariness (alphabetical order is the classic declaration: an ordering so obviously external that no one reads rank into it), and the compositional lie is the imposition of a fake organism — a narrative arc troweled over a heap, manufacturing the second-direction divergence of §7. On Truth licenses this inversion explicitly: piecemeal logic "is adequate in those instances in which the inner functional content approaches zero." For zero-functional-content substance, piecemeal composition is not a compromise; it is the correct form, and pretending otherwise is tF.

**The radical thesis, assessed.** Is substance, then, sometimes "not essentially linear or sequential at all, better represented as a simultaneous configuration than as a linear sequence"? The material forces a differentiated yes. Yes: the achieved structure is often chordal — the Gauss symmetry, the web of mutual determination, the matrix — and for these, linear text stands to the substance as an *arpeggio* stands to a chord: the simultaneity spread in time. But the melody phenomenology blocks the despairing conclusion. Temporal wholes are genuine wholes; the spreading of a chord in time remains *hearable as the chord* provided the spreading respects the harmony and the listener's retention is fed — which is precisely what closure, recurrence, similarity-bridging, and owed resolutions are for. And the deeper point cuts underneath the dichotomy: even chordal substance is only ever *grasped* in a temporal act. The simultaneity belongs to the structure; the grasping is a process; and the text was never in the business of *depicting* the structure — it is a score for the grasping. Its office is choreography, not portraiture. What linearity strictly forbids is only simultaneity of *presentation*; simultaneity of *organization* is reconstituted on the reader's side, and composition succeeds exactly when it gives retention and closure enough to do that work. The residue — substances whose mutual dependencies exceed what suspension can carry — is real, and there the limit of the essay-form is itself a finding about the substance, to be stated rather than papered over.

## 9. Consequences for an instrument of writing

The analysis was undertaken partly with an instrument in view, and its design consequences now write themselves, so they can be brief. Such a tool would hold **W₁ and W₂ as distinct, co-edited layers**, with the mapping between them a first-class, inspectable object — because every diagnosis above is a predicate of the mapping, not of either layer alone. From W₁ it would derive the **partial order of process-requirements** (gap-before-filling, set-before-material, overthrown-view-before-recentering, debt-before-payment) and present the space of admissible extensions, distinguishing necessary precedences from arbitrary residue — offering to optimize the residue or to declare it. Against a chosen extension it would run the diagnostics this essay has named: **false adjacencies** (textual neighbors without a functional edge), **uncovered broken adjacencies** (functional edges spanning textual distance without a similarity-bridge or recurrence), **premature resolutions** (S₂ preceding its S₁), **empty bridges** (transitions certifying only adjacency), **undeclared heaps and overdressed heaps**, **unmarked 93° deviations** from canonical schemata, and **unpaid IOUs** at document end. And it would treat **recentering as a primary operation on W₁** that re-derives the partial order, flags the extension for re-sequencing, and — expecting the jolt — audits surviving blocks for changed function under preserved wording, since the homotypy test says those are precisely the blocks whose letters can no longer honestly be the same. Above all it would embody the asymmetry thesis: the argument-layer is the whole for whose sake the text-layer exists, and a writer who grasps configurations whole but struggles to serialize them is not deficient in the substance of writing — he is confronting, without instruments, exactly the mapping problem this essay has mapped.

## 10. Coda

This essay's own substance is a small web: two applications of one distinction, four sources, two contexts, one mapping-space, everything bearing on everything. It could not present that web; it chose a linear extension. Its necessary precedences it believes it obeyed — the distinction before the asymmetry, the asymmetry before the truth-aptness that presupposes it, the reader-side mechanics before the mapping that spends them, the poles before the space. Its arbitrary components it ordered by the reading sequence, declaring hereby that the order of §§3–6 tracks the order of the texts, not a hierarchy among them. It issued IOUs — footnote 17 was flagged in §4 and redeemed in §8 — and it has paid them. Its proximity lies where every source-by-source exposition's do: the four source-sections sit side by side as siblings, though their contents interlock as a cycle, and the similarity of their construction was this text's bridge over that break. And by its own account its substance is a recentering — from "the part–whole distinction applies to arguments *and* to texts," an empty and, two topics in mere coexistence, to their interlocking: the text as the field whose organizing forces are the argument's only way into a mind. If the essay has worked, that "and" no longer reads as it did in §1 — which, since statements have a direction in their context, is exactly what it was for.
